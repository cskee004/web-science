      <div class="hl__full-template__page-content hl__full-template__page-content--stacked" id="about" tabindex="-1"><div class="hl__comp-header" tab-index="-1"><h2 class="hl__comp-heading ">  About the Poetry Room
</h2></div></div>  
  





<div class="hl__full-template__page-content hl__full-template__page-content--stacked hl__full-template__page-content--sub-row" id="rich_text-421" tabindex="-1">
        <section class="hl__show-more-text"><div  id="showMoreText421"><section class="hl__rich-text"><p class="hl__rte-large">Welcome to the Woodberry Poetry Room, a special collections reading room and lively literary center at Harvard University.&nbsp;</p><figure class="hl__figure--right hl__figure"><img loading="lazy" src="/sites/default/files/styles/content_embed/public/Welcome2000section_DorotheaLaskyvisitsthePoetryRoom_0.jpg?itok=h2TB67Vk" width="450" height="253" alt="Welcome to the Poetry Room featuring a photo of poet Dorothea Lasky" class="hl__image"><figcaption class="hl__figure__figcaption"><div class="hl__figure__text-caption">
                Dorothea Lasky at the Poetry Room

                          </div></figcaption></figure><p>Located in Lamont Library — in a room designed by <a href="https://www.alvaraalto.fi/en/">Alvar Aalto</a> — and overseen by Houghton Library, the Poetry Room features over 5,000 poetry monographs and magazines and a landmark collection of literary recordings (1933-present).</p><p>With over 6,000 recordings on a range of media that span the 20th and 21st centuries — including discs, magnetic tape (reel to reel and cassette), CDs, DATs, and born digital — the collection is one of the largest and earliest literary sound archives in the United States.</p><p>&nbsp;</p><h2>BRIEF HISTORY OF "THE LIBRARY OF VOICES"</h2><h3><br>
Woodberry at Widener: 1931—1949</h3><figure class="hl__figure--left hl__figure"><img loading="lazy" src="/sites/default/files/styles/content_embed/public/Welcome_Poetry%20Room_WidenerLibrary_undated1940s.jpg?itok=jnqhGHoh" width="450" height="253" alt="Poetry Room at Widener Library with a group of people standing and looking at the camera" class="hl__image"><figcaption class="hl__figure__figcaption"><div class="hl__figure__text-caption">
                Poetry Room at Widener Library (1931)

                          </div></figcaption></figure><p>Founded in honor of poet, scholar, and Harvard graduate&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_Edward_Woodberry">George Edward Woodberry (1855-1930)</a>, the Poetry Room first opened its doors on the third floor of Widener Library in 1931. Prof. Harry Levin later reflected that its opening was a kind of declaration that "Harvard was officially recognizing modern poetry."</p><p>The early Poetry Room featured seminal materials from the&nbsp;<a href="http://nrs.harvard.edu/urn-3:FHCL.Hough:hou01012">Amy Lowell collection</a>, now a part of Houghton Library, as well as Modernist monographs and magazines. The&nbsp;<a href="https://hollis.harvard.edu/primo-explore/search?query=any,contains,%22morris%20gray%22&amp;amp=&amp;amp=&amp;amp=&amp;amp=&amp;amp=&amp;amp=&amp;amp=&amp;amp=&amp;amp=&amp;tab=everything&amp;search_scope=everything&amp;sortby=rank&amp;vid=HVD2&amp;facet=rtype,include,sound_recordings&amp;lang=en_US&amp;offset=0">Morris Gray lecture series</a>&nbsp;— which hosted such luminaries as Martha Dickinson Bianchi, Robert Frost, Wallace Stevens, and William Carlos Williams — added an audible dimension to the room. In 1938, a Victrola was installed, and phonograph records swiftly became the fulcrum of the room and its collections.</p><p>&nbsp;</p><h3>The Harvard Vocarium: 1933—1955</h3><figure class="hl__figure--right hl__figure"><img loading="lazy" src="/sites/default/files/styles/content_embed/public/Eliot%20disc%20edited_0.jpg?itok=gfoJ-Quj" width="450" height="440" alt="Harvard Vocarium vinyl view with label" class="hl__image"><figcaption class="hl__figure__figcaption"><div class="hl__figure__text-caption">
                Harvard Vocarium disc

                          </div></figcaption></figure><p>The mere presence of phonograph records does not explain how the Poetry Room became a landmark A/V collection. For that, we need to turn to the pioneering work of Harvard Professor of Public Speaking <a href="http://nrs.harvard.edu/urn-3:FHCL.Hough:hou02820">Frederick C. Packard, Jr.</a>.<br><br>
As early as 1929, Packard began to clamor for the creation of the world's first-ever library of voices (a "vocarium," as he and Prof. E. K. Rand dubbed it), which would be “a place where voices can be kept and studied” and stand in equal stature to a library of books. He found a home for his vision in the nascent Poetry Room: “My Vocarium was in<em> there.</em>”<br>
&nbsp;</p>




A repository of all of the voices he could get a hold of, a kind of audio time-capsule....

Josephine Packard<br>
&nbsp;


<p>In 1933, Packard launched the Harvard Vocarium, one of the first poetry and spoken-literature recording labels in the world. The preliminary batch featured several Latin and Greek recordings and <a href="https://www.harvard.edu/in-focus/poetry/#eliot">T. S. Eliot's first poetry recording</a>.&nbsp;Eliot's recording of "Gerontion" and "The Hollow Men"&nbsp;was made by Packard during the poet's year-long Charles Eliot Norton lecturership.</p><p>Until its discontinuation in 1955, the Harvard Vocarium made and, in many cases commercially released, the first (or earliest extant) recordings by such authors as <strong>Elizabeth Bishop, Robert Lowell, Marianne Moore, Vladimir Nabokov, Anais Nin, Ezra Pound</strong>, and<strong> Tennessee Williams.</strong>&nbsp;While a 1938&nbsp;<em>Boston Sunday Post</em>&nbsp;article boasted that “Harvard University, one of the most forward universities in the world, is breaking all precedent and <strong>founding a library for the voice</strong>,” the vast library (encompassing poetry readings, theatrical performances, musical concerts, classical oratory, and political speeches) that Packard envisioned never wholly materialized. But his literary recordings <em>did </em>continue to be deposited, preserved, and accessed at the Woodberry Poetry Room, where an active effort to catalog and preserve them continues to this day.</p><p>&nbsp;</p><h3>The Poetry Room in Lamont Library: 1949—present</h3><p>By the late 1940s, the venue had become so popular that plans were made to move it to the new Lamont Library.</p><figure class="hl__figure--left hl__figure"><img loading="lazy" src="/sites/default/files/styles/content_embed/public/Welcome_Cartoon.jpg?itok=E_MufbqY" width="450" height="253" alt="Cartoon of students listening to records in the Lamont Poetry Room" class="hl__image"></figure><p>When the Poetry Room opened in Lamont in&nbsp;1949, it was in a new suite of&nbsp;<a href="http://id.lib.harvard.edu/aleph/015129373/catalog">Alvar Aalto-designed rooms</a>&nbsp;created specifically as "a place for poetry" and poetry listening. Four Thorens turntables, equipped with outlets for eight sets of earphones, helped to accommodate the growing visitorship. Over 4,000 listening requests were made during its first four months alone.</p><p>(The downside of this move was that it excluded women from the Poetry Room: Radcliffe students had previously had access to its Widener venue. With the exception of summer sessions, during which time such poets as Adrienne Rich and Sylvia Plath were able to visit, Lamont Library did not admit women until 1967.)</p><p>During <strong>John Lincoln Sweeney</strong>’s mid-century curatorship, the Poetry Room flourished and became a nexus for poets who were (or would later be) associated with the New York School of Poets, the Boston Renaissance, and the so-called "Confessional" poetry movement.&nbsp;The Poetry Room also forged a significant collaboration "The Poet Speaks" with the British Council, which jointly funded recordings by Kamau Brathwaite, Philip Larkin, Hugh MacDiarmid, Stevie Smith, Ted Hughes, among others.</p><figure class="hl__figure--left hl__figure"><img loading="lazy" src="/sites/default/files/styles/content_embed/public/Folksingers-Master-reel-768x598_0.jpg?itok=_ypvfTCV" width="450" height="350" alt="Fassett Studio reel of Joan Baez" class="hl__image"><figcaption class="hl__figure__figcaption"><div class="hl__figure__text-caption">
                Fassett Studio reel of Joan Baez

                          </div></figcaption></figure><p>Working in collaboration with Packard and with the expertise of sound engineer&nbsp;<a href="http://id.lib.harvard.edu/ead/hou02818/catalog">Stephen B. Fassett</a>, Sweeney was responsible for documenting the circuit tours of E. E. Cummings and Dylan Thomas, for making <a href="https://library.harvard.edu/sites/default/files/static/poetry/listeningbooth/poets/stevens.html">Wallace Stevens'</a> last recordings, and for chronicling the performances of such emerging mid-century writers as John Ashbery, John Berryman, Robert Creeley, Allen Ginsberg, Stephen Jonas, Robert Lowell,&nbsp;<a href="http://id.lib.harvard.edu/aleph/001788919/catalog">Sylvia Plath</a>, Adrienne Rich, Anne Sexton, and John Wieners.</p><p>Sweeney also facilitated and recorded literary conferences and folk-music performances in Lamont Forum Room: among the highlights from this collection are one of the earliest live recordings of&nbsp;<a href="http://id.lib.harvard.edu/aleph/001695406/catalog">Ralph Ellison</a>, made during the Conference on the Contemporary Novel, in August 1953.</p><p>Fassett continued to assist interim curators after Sweeney's tenure: in addition to his early work recording everyone from Baez to Plath, Fassett&nbsp;was instrumental in making Poetry Room recordings by <a href="https://library.harvard.edu/sites/default/files/static/poetry/listeningbooth/poets/lorde.html">Audre Lorde</a> and Yvegeny Yevtushenko.</p><figure class="hl__figure--right hl__figure"><img loading="lazy" src="/sites/default/files/styles/content_embed/public/Welcome_Heaney.jpg?itok=A4vpui5y" width="450" height="253" alt="Seamus Heaney beside his portrait in the Poetry Room" class="hl__image"><figcaption class="hl__figure__figcaption"><div class="hl__figure__text-caption">
                Seamus Heaney at the Poetry Room

                          </div></figcaption></figure><p>In 1974, <strong>Stratis Haviaras </strong>was named curator of the Poetry and Farnsworth Rooms, a position he held until 2000. During this lively period, Haviaras recorded a wide array of poets representing a broad range of late 20th century poetics: among them, Charles Bernstein, Rita Dove, Robert Duncan, Jorie Graham, Galway Kinnell, Sharon Olds, Gary Snyder, and Derek Walcott. Haviaras was also responsible for creating a substantive archive of Greek-language recordings, featuring such authors as George Seferis and Odysseus Elytis.<br><br>
In addition, he recorded over 40 readings, seminars, and lectures by <a href="https://library.harvard.edu/sites/default/files/static/poetry/listeningbooth/poets/heaney.html">Seamus Heaney</a>, during Heaney's pivotal period of affiliation with the university. Like Sweeney before him, Haviaras also began to transfer the Poetry Room's recordings to the next iteration of sound recording: the compact cassette.</p><p>In 1986, Haviaras founded the literary journal&nbsp;<em>Errato</em>, which later became the&nbsp;<em><a href="http://www.harvardreview.org/">Harvard Review</a></em>--contributors to which have included Jhumpa Lahiri, J. M. Coetzee, Yusef Komunyakaa, David Foster Wallace, and the over 200 poets who participated in the recent&nbsp;<em>Renga for Obama</em>.</p><p>&nbsp;</p><h3>The Poetry Room Joins Houghton Library: circa 2000</h3><figure class="hl__figure--left hl__figure"><img loading="lazy" src="/sites/default/files/styles/content_embed/public/ClaudiaRankine.jpg?itok=TEOWSHnj" width="450" height="279" alt="Claudia Rankine reads from CITIZEN at a podium" class="hl__image"><figcaption class="hl__figure__figcaption"><div class="hl__figure__text-caption">
                Claudia Rankine reads for the Poetry Room

                          </div></figcaption></figure><p>With Haviaras’ retirement in 2000, the Poetry Room was transferred to management under Houghton Library: its landmark recording collection having been deemed a special collection.</p><p>In 2002, during the dynamic curatorship of <strong>Don Share,</strong> the Harvard Vocarium was chosen by the Library of Congress as a part of the first annual selection of&nbsp;the <a href="https://www.loc.gov/programs/static/national-recording-preservation-board/documents/Harvard%20Vocarium%20essay.pdf">National Recording Registry</a>.<br><br>
Share was also crucial in recognizing the precarity of early recording formats and helped pilot the first Library Digitization Initiative at Harvard, establishing new standards for audio preservation. In 2006, Share joined the staff of&nbsp;<em>Poetry</em>&nbsp;Magazine.</p>




[The Poetry Room] contains not only the voices of the greatest poets, but constitutes a living history of modern poetry....

Seamus Heaney<br>
&nbsp;


<p>The Poetry Room has a long history of hiring poets and writers, and the current staff is no exception to that legacy. <strong>Christina Davis</strong> and <strong>Mary Walker Graham</strong> are both actively publishing poets. Past curatorial assistants and Harvard students who have worked at the Poetry Room include: Chloe Garcia Roberts, Maureen McLane, Fred Moten, and Lindsay Turner.</p><p>Poetry Room curators from 1931 to the present have been: W. N. Bates, George M. Kahrl, Arthur T. Hamlin, Arnold M. Kenseth, Philip Horton, John Lincoln Sweeney, Robert Fitzgerald, Jeanne Broburg, Stratis Haviaras, Don Share, and Christina Davis.<br><br>
The Woodberry Poetry Room's A/V collection is, according to Nobel laureate Seamus Heaney, "indispensable: it contains not only the voices—from different times of their lives—of the greatest poets, but constitutes a living history of modern poetry." This vital tradition continues today with a rich assortment of <a href="https://www.youtube.com/channel/UCx7LgX1FZbs7zguHTxGmPKw">readings and performances,</a> as well as significant efforts to preserve and digitize the&nbsp;<a href="/collections/listening-booth">Poetry Room’s pivotal recordings</a>&nbsp;for generations to come.</p><p>&nbsp;</p></section></div></section>  </div>
